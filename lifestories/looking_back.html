Where is Happiness?

Who really Loves You?

My best friend was calling. He had been the first person I met when I moved to Colorado, and that was over 30 years ago.

“Hey Drew, how’s it going!”

“Awwh, its pretty good man. How bout you? How are things in Texas?”

“Hot as usual.” We both laughed and continued catching up as we had done many times since we were kids in 1st grade. But as we finished syncing up, he said, “So I got some bad news.”

“Oh?”

“Well, my sister and her husband split up.”

“Oh, no. Sorry to hear that man. Is Hannah alright?”

“Well, that’s what I was going to ask you. Would you mind giving her a call? Cause I know you’ve kinda been through something similar before, and maybe you could just help her to see the light at the end of the tunnel, ya know?”

“Sure thing man. Ill give her a call tonight.”

And so I did.

“Hey Hannah, its Andrew”

“Oh! Hi Andrew.”

“Hey, I heard about Corey and was wondering if you wanted to talk for a bit?”

And so we talked and I listened as she told me about what had happened that led to their divorce. She was devastated and she had moved back closer to her family.

“So although I haven’t been divorced, would you care to listen to a breakup I have been through?”

“Sure.”

“Okay, here goes.”

It was summer and I was a thousand miles away from my family in the hot and suffocating humidity of Texas working as an intern. I had just gotten off the phone with my girlfriend who I had been dating for almost three years; since I was a freshmen. She was also working as an intern but in a small, Arizona mining town, and although we were split by distance, we had survived previous summers by keeping in touch over the phone. I had not expected this summer to be any different. That is, until she stopped answering my calls or text messages. At first I thought that she was just busy, but slowly doubt entered my mind as the weeks slipped by, until finally, I received the call from her.

Although I had cried when we talked, a few hours after the phone call, a second wave of grief hit me. I laid face down across my bed, barely breathing into the pillow that I hoped would suffocate me as I laid motionless, pain welling up behind my eyes as I had lost any capability of producing any more tears. I closed them, hoping to be struck by lightning, but instead I was whisked away into my memories.

We were holding hands as we crossed a bridge over Clear Creek, its wooden panels gently creaked beneath our footsteps. Snow that had once locked the ground in its frozen vice was now slowly receding away and the river was exploding with life. With cameras in hand, we climbed down to the river, and I helped her down the steep slope, carefully watching her every move. We stepped to the edge of the rock bed at the bend of the river, and she began taking a picture of an upright leaf half frozen in ice catching the sunlight’s reflections in a million different colors. I watched her with adoration as she gracefully perfected her camera position; her gorgeous blond hair was gold in the sunlight, and her complexion was smoother than the river rocks that surrounded her. She turned to see me watching and smiled at me. I was transfixed by her big smile and those gentle, pink lips. I smiled back, love struck.

The moment escaped me as I slowly sat up on the bed, weak from not eating. I wasn’t even sure why it had happened. She said she had changed. Said she wasn’t the same anymore and her new Arizona friends had shown her new things. She said she didn’t care much for religious things anymore and thought that a Catholic like me should find someone better. Said I deserved better. But I didn’t believe her. After that first day, I kept in touch thinking that it was all just a game and that as soon as next semester started, things would be back to the way they were: two people still madly in love. My mother didn’t think the same. After I had told her what had happened, she of course comforted me, but told me that she was probably right and I would find someone better. Although I agreed, I didn’t believe it and didn’t mention to my mother that I still loved her. I also didn’t tell her my biggest fear: that I would never find anyone else. After all, I figured she was the only person who had understood me so well.

The semester following that summer came slowly. But eventually, it came, and the only thing I had on my mind was to see her again. I was certain that was all we needed to bring us back to life. We met outside one of the campus buildings and sat in the grass, and immediately I sensed what I feared. She had changed. She still had the same beautiful figure and gentle voice that I had learned to love, but now she felt distanced. Although I sat not two feet from her in the grass, she was farther away than we had ever been. I wanted to hold her but dared not break the invisible barrier between us. She told me again what she had told me over and over during the summer. She no longer wanted to be with anyone and wanted to travel the world as she visited all the different mines she would one day work at. She said she no longer believed in God and told me to let her go... I deserved someone better than her. I asked her if she still loved me and her response was, “I do, Andrew, but we were not meant to be together. You need to move on.” It made sense logically, but my emotions were still not in agreement. I would have traded everything for a time machine to go back to what once was.

We were studying late together and had gotten hungry as it was late. Thankfully, King Soopers was open 24/7 and we adventured out with some of my other roommates in search of food. The guys bought things like Gatorade or Redbull or chips, but I bought her two favorite things: strawberries and Jet-Puffed marshmallow cream. We got back to the dorm and I went to open the jar of marshmallow puff. I tried grabbing one of the three small, foil tabs to remove the seal but only succeeded in getting a small portion off and the marshmallow began exploding out of the small opening onto my fingers. She began laughing and asked for a try, so I passed the jar. While she was preoccupied with it, I quickly marked a dot on her soft cheek with my marshmallowed finger. She stopped and looked at me, half in shock, half smiling, and while she was stunned, I matched the dot with a similarly placed one on her other cheek. Now I was laughing. Of course she didn’t let me off that easy and mashed a handful of cream onto my face. We were now both laughing and taking turns marking up each other voluntarily. My roommates stood in the doorway, shaking their heads but each amused at our game. Ryan then said, “Geez, you guys are perfect for each other,” with a hint of jealously.

For at least a week after, I didn’t give up on our relationship and sent her messages saying how much I missed her. But about a month or so after we first saw each other after the summer, she began not talking to me. She said she could no longer stand my messages because I was hurting her. Yet in my sickness, I was almost happy that I had caused her pain. I wanted her to feel the same pain I felt. The same nagging feeling of something pulling you farther and farther into a deeper and deeper pit. After all these years of sharing happiness, I figured we should now share the same pain. I kept with this belief for several days until a thought crept into my head. What if she was already having a hard time and reminding her of the pain she caused me might be too much to bear? What if she went so far as to end her life? I thought of her parents and her roommate. They would be distraught. Even worse, what if they knew I had been the reason, because I could not let go? With this in mind, I resolved to never think of her again and deleted her number from my phone, yet I kept it written on a paper in my drawer, just in case we ever did get back together.

But forgetting was easier said than done as I was reminded of her everywhere; from finding pieces of her hair on my jacket, to making spaghetti (which I made for her all the time since she liked it), to leafing through my photography and finding not only pictures of her, but also the pictures that she had taken alongside of me. Not only that, but my four-year-old sister had grown found of her and nearly every single time I went to my parents house she would ask where she was so they could play together. With all these reminders, I fell back into the past, losing my presence in the world. Sure I still went to class and saw my family at home, but to me everything appeared dull. It was as if a veil had been put over the earth and I was only seeing things in black and white where there had once been color. Once where there had been an energetic guy who enjoyed life, was a zombie going through the movements of life. I myself noticed these changes and tried to fix it, whatever that meant. I went to some parties with friends, and I never turned down an invitation from my roommates to do something. I went out often with them, if only to go to a restaurant like Old Chicagos, but even after an eventful day with friends, when I would come back home and slow down, she would enter into my thoughts like a their in the night only to leave me with nothing but dreams of what once was as I sobbed myself to sleep.

We hiked in the glorious sun along the river at Castle Wood Canyon. It was an easy trail and we made many stops along the river to take pictures of the blossoming, spring flowers that speckled color along the sides of the river bed. We laughed together at the crawdads who darted away at the hint of our arrival and we told stories of our childhoods when we were young and tried to catch various critters in our backyards. We hiked to the sparkling waterfall at the end of the trail and laid in the grass; her head dozing up against my chest. She said she liked to listen to my heart and found its beat soothing, and I told her that was because that’s where she belongs.. close to my heart.

I couldn’t take it any longer. Living in the past was slowly draining me into a lifeless gray and questions constantly haunted me. Was there a different reason she left me? Was I not good enough? Had I done something wrong? Or was it my personality? I pondered these things and again the thought came to me that I would never find another. I was doomed to wander life, never to love again. To shake these thoughts, one day I took a hike up South Table Mountain. It was a beautiful day outside with not so much as a breeze to chase away the sun’s warmth. I hiked briskly up the winding trail, relishing the chance to challenge myself and keep my mind preoccupied on something else. Others were on the trail that morning as well, and as I said hello to each one of these other groups, I began to notice that nearly every one of them were married couples. Some were young, some were old, some of them in between with kids doing circles around them. I saw their happiness and was once again reminded of my own loneliness and what I had lost. Almost as if to spite these others, I stopped greeting them. I arrived at the top of hill, and marched right up to the precarious rock outcropping that overlooked the city. My legs grew cold as I looked down over the cliff. It welcomed me and I thought of accepting its kind invitation. Its offer was tempting. No longer would this haunting love pursue me. No longer would I have to deal with this cold world that refused to love me back. All I had to do was to let my legs buckle beneath the weight that had become too heavy and I would step through death’s door to never look back. But as I thought of stepping through the door, I looked back and I saw my family, my friends and coworkers whose love I had not considered. Finally, I thought of the possibility of a future lover who would never get the chance if I stepped through that door. I hesitated, looked over the city and then smiled as I imagined someone down there. So I stepped back and with a different energy bounded back down the mountain.

When I arrived back at my apartment, I realized I had left my stereo on. It was a bad habit of mine, but when I went to go turn it off, I heard a song by Passenger. The song name was, “Let her go”:

Well you see her when you fall asleep
But never to touch and never to keep
’Cause you loved her too much
And you dived too deep

Well you only need the light when it’s burning low
Only miss the sun when it starts to snow
Only know you love her when you let her go

Only know you’ve been high when you’re feeling low
Only hate the road when you’re missin’ home
Only know you love her when you let her go

And you let her go
And you let her go
Will you let her go?

I heard silence at the end of the phone line. Finally, Hannah spoke, “I like that song.”

“Me too,” I said. “It kinda goes to show that sometimes a relationship gets too overbearing. It then takes loosing that relationship to realize how much other people around you love you and that really it’s our friends and family that we should always hold onto.”

“That’s so true. It’s been amazing how everyone around me, including you, have been so supporting as of late. It’s really been uplifting.”
